# Other Philanthropic and Non-Profit Funders of Pandemic Preparedness and Infectious-Disease Work

*Compiled 22 June 2026. Source-backed profiles of philanthropies active — to varying degrees — in pandemic preparedness, infectious disease, and global health. Figures and years are drawn from primary sources (organisation websites, press releases) and reputable news; uncertain or interpretive points are flagged explicitly. Grantees listed are representative, not exhaustive.*

---

## Introduction

Beyond the large, dedicated global-health institutions (Wellcome, the Gates Foundation, the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative, CEPI/Gavi), a varied second tier of philanthropies touches pandemic preparedness and infectious-disease research. They differ widely in approach: some fund basic biomedical science (HHMI), some fund people and data systems rather than R&D (Bloomberg, Resolve to Save Lives, the Trinity Challenge), some are tech-founder vehicles backing AI-for-science and biosecurity (Schmidt, McGovern, Flu Lab), and some are catalytic funding platforms or regionally focused funders (the Audacious Project, the ELMA Foundation). A few names commonly cited in this space (e.g. the Templeton foundations) turn out to have minimal genuine pandemic relevance. The profiles below note scale, programmes, and representative grantees, with explicit flags where claims could not be substantiated.

---

## 1. Howard Hughes Medical Institute (HHMI)

HHMI is one of the world's largest private funders of basic biomedical research, with consolidated net assets of roughly **$24.2 billion at the end of FY2023** (its endowment is widely cited at ~$22.6 billion) and an annual research investment of roughly **$800–825 million**. Its defining model is the **Investigator Program** — funding "people, not projects." Rather than awarding project grants, HHMI appoints scientists as HHMI Investigators and pays full salary plus long-term lab support on renewable multi-year terms, freeing them to pursue high-risk work. Investigators remain based at their home universities. The model is associated with 35 Nobel laureates.

A substantial share of HHMI investigators work on infectious disease and immunology. Representative names include **David Veesler** (coronavirus structural biology), **Akiko Iwasaki** (antiviral and mucosal immunity, Yale), **Jean-Laurent Casanova** (inborn errors of immunity underlying severe infection), **Jonathan Abraham** (emerging viruses, Harvard), **Gabriel Victora** (antibody responses), **Daniel Mucida** (mucosal immunology), and **Nels Elde** (virus–host evolution).

The **Emerging Pathogens Initiative is real and confirmed** (this was a key verification point). In **January 2023**, HHMI committed **$100 million** to the initiative to support basic research giving "a scientific head start on future epidemics" (the phrasing of HHMI Chief Scientific Officer Leslie Vosshall). It funds **13 project teams comprising roughly 70 scientists across 29 institutions**. Named participants include Richard Flavell, Anna Pyle, Akiko Iwasaki and Paul Turner (Yale), and Raphael Valdivia (Duke). *(Flag: some sources describe it as a roughly three-year initiative, but the exact term length was not stated consistently across primary sources — treat the duration as soft.)*

During COVID-19, HHMI investigators pivoted heavily into pandemic-relevant work — testing, drug repurposing, durability of immunity, and the cytokine storm. *(Flag: it is consistent with HHMI's program design that investigators and lab staff continued to be paid through 2020 lab shutdowns, since HHMI provides full salary plus lab support, but I could not retrieve a primary HHMI statement explicitly committing to "no layoffs / continued pay" during the pandemic — HHMI's website blocked automated retrieval. Treat that specific commitment as unverified.)*

## 2. Bloomberg Philanthropies

Bloomberg Philanthropies' public-health programme targets preventable deaths from **tobacco use, road traffic crashes, non-communicable diseases (NCDs), lead and other environmental exposures, and gaps in reproductive and emergency care** — working primarily through cities and low- and middle-income countries. Its model emphasises proven policy and implementation rather than pandemic vaccine/drug R&D. *(Flag: the "not pandemic R&D" framing is an accurate characterisation of Bloomberg's stated priorities, but it is interpretive — Bloomberg does not phrase it that way itself.)* Michael R. Bloomberg has served since 2016 as **WHO Global Ambassador for Noncommunicable Diseases** (later expanded to NCDs and Injuries). Since 2005, Bloomberg has committed roughly **$1.6 billion** to global tobacco control.

Its most data-relevant programme is **Data for Health**, launched **March 2015** as a **$100 million** initiative — co-funded with the Australian government and the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation — to help 20 low- and middle-income countries strengthen public-health data, particularly **civil registration and vital statistics (CRVS)**. It has since expanded to **40+ countries** through its Global Grants Program, with more than **28 million birth/death records** newly collected or improved and over **120,000 health professionals trained** since 2015. Key implementing partners include **Vital Strategies, the CDC Foundation, the Global Health Advocacy Incubator, Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health, PAHO**, and UN regional commissions.

Bloomberg's most direct link to epidemic preparedness is as a **founding funder of Resolve to Save Lives** (2017), alongside the Gates Foundation and the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative (see §7).

## 3. The Trinity Challenge

The Trinity Challenge (TTC) is a **UK-registered charity** (Charity No. 1191909) that supports the creation of **data-driven and analytics solutions to protect against global health emergencies**. It was **launched on 14 September 2020** as a COVID-era coalition of business, academic, and philanthropic institutions, and is convened and chaired by **Dame Sally Davies** — Master of Trinity College Cambridge, former Chief Medical Officer for England, and UK Special Envoy on Antimicrobial Resistance.

**Founding members (corrected from common assumptions):** the September 2020 launch named roughly **20 founding members**, including the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, University of Cambridge, **Google, Tencent, McKinsey & Company**, Microsoft, Facebook, Imperial College London, LSE, the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation, Optum, Reckitt Benckiser, Legal & General, Aviva, and the Global Virome Project. *(Flags: (a) Wellcome was NOT a founding member — it is a later "Funding Member"; (b) GSK appears in some aggregated listings but not in the Cambridge launch release, so its founding status is unverified; (c) the occasionally cited "~42 institutions" figure is not supported by primary sources and appears to conflate later membership growth.)* Current funding members include Wellcome, MSD, Novo Nordisk Fonden, the Pfizer Foundation, the INEOS Oxford Institute, the Institute of Philanthropy, and the **Patrick J. McGovern Foundation** (see §8).

TTC's flagship is its **prize-challenge** model:

- **Challenge 1 — "Protect against future health emergencies" (announced 2021):** launched with a £10m headline ambition; ultimately awarded **£5.7 million (~US$8M)** across 8 winners, from 340 applications across ~60 countries. **Grand prize: PODD** (Participatory One Health Disease Detection, Thailand) — £1.3m for a livestock-disease reporting app to prevent zoonotic spillover. Second prizes (£1m each): the **Sentinel Forecasting System** (Kate Jones, UCL) and **BloodCounts!** (Cambridge). Five third prizes (~£480k each) including **MedShr, Khushi Health, Living Goods, VaccineLedger**, and a UMass Lowell sensor-network project.
- **Challenge 2 — Antimicrobial Resistance (2024):** **£2.7 million** across 4 teams (285 applications, 57 countries). Grand prize: **Farm2Vet** (Vietnam, £1m); joint second **AMRSense** and **OASIS** (India, £600k each); third **AMRoots** (South Africa, £500k).
- **Challenge 3 — Community Access to Effective Antibiotics (2025):** **£1 million**, two winners at £500k each — **Com-WATCH** (Nigeria) and **PADs / Paper Analytical Devices** (multi-country).

## 4. Schmidt Futures / Eric & Wendy Schmidt

The philanthropic vehicles of former Google CEO Eric Schmidt and Wendy Schmidt were **reorganised in 2024**, when **Schmidt Sciences** was spun out of Schmidt Futures as a new charitable organisation (led by President and Chief Scientist Dr. Stuart Feldman) covering AI and Advanced Computing, Astrophysics and Space, Biosciences, Climate, and Cross-Science Programs. It operates alongside the family's other entities — **Schmidt Futures, the Schmidt Family Foundation, and the Schmidt Ocean Institute**.

The Schmidts' signature science work is heavily **AI-for-science** focused. **AI2050** — conceived and co-chaired by Eric Schmidt and James Manyika, now a Schmidt Sciences program — represents a **$125 million, five-year** commitment funding cohorts of Early Career and Senior Fellows. Related gifts include a **$150 million** endowment to create the **Eric and Wendy Schmidt Center at the Broad Institute** (March 2021, matched by $150m from the Broad Foundation) to merge biology and machine learning, and a $1.95m gift to Mount Sinai (2024).

**Brown University Pandemic Center — UNVERIFIED / likely a name conflation.** I could not find any primary source or reputable news confirming a Schmidt gift to the **Pandemic Center at Brown University's School of Public Health** (founded October 2022; director Jennifer Nuzzo). Brown's Center is consistently described as launched through Brown's "BrownTogether" campaign, and its documented external funders are different entities — **Open Philanthropy (now Coefficient Giving), the Carnegie Corporation (~$900k), and the Peter G. Peterson Foundation**. The most likely explanation is conflation of **Brown** with the **Broad** Institute's Schmidt Center. **The "Schmidt funded the Brown Pandemic Center" claim should be treated as unverified** absent a specific announcement.

## 5. The Flu Lab

The Flu Lab is a philanthropic organisation **founded in 2018** with the mission of **"a world free from the threat of influenza."** It works through three mechanisms: grantmaking, early-stage investing, and partner collaborations. It was **founded and funded by Lucinda ("Lucy") Southworth**, wife of Google co-founder Larry Page, who is reported as the financial backer; the entities are deliberately low-disclosure, so totals are not public. Current leadership includes CEO Casey Wright (former CDC/HHS) and CTO Julie Schafer (former BARDA and NSC).

On the grantmaking side, Flu Lab's most notable initiative was a **2019 partnership with the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation** to fund **six grants of up to $2 million each over two years** toward a **universal flu vaccine**; named grantees include leading influenza researcher **Yoshihiro Kawaoka** (University of Tokyo / University of Wisconsin–Madison). On the investment side it has backed companies such as **Vivaldi Biosciences** (the DeltaFLU nasal-spray universal influenza vaccine). Its broader portfolio spans vaccines, surveillance, and advocacy. *(Flag: the "up to $2M over two years" figure was reported via the Gates side of the partnership; Flu Lab's own contribution amounts and Larry Page's total funding are not disclosed.)*

## 6. Gates Ventures vs. the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation

These are frequently confused but are entirely distinct. **Gates Ventures** is **Bill Gates's private personal office** (an LLC-type private vehicle, founded 2008, HQ in Kirkland, WA; formerly bgC3 / Carillon Holdings, rebranded 2018). It is **not** a charity: it houses his personal staff, a private think tank on health and global development, and a technology investment portfolio, and it runs the *Gates Notes* site and supports his books and personal projects. It operates with limited public disclosure. By contrast, the **Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation** (launched 2000, Seattle) is the public-reporting charitable foundation that funds global health — including vaccines, **CEPI**, and **Gavi**.

On pandemic R&D, the **Foundation** (not Gates Ventures) is the relevant actor: with Wellcome it pledged **US$300 million to CEPI** (US$150m each, January 2022) for COVID response and future pandemic preparedness, made multiple ACT-Accelerator/COVAX-era commitments in 2020, and pledged **$1.6 billion to Gavi** (reported 2025) for childhood vaccines. The pandemic ideas associated with **Bill Gates personally** — his 2022 book *How to Prevent the Next Pandemic* and its proposed **GERM (Global Epidemic Response and Mobilization) team** (~3,000 experts, WHO-managed, ~$1bn/year) — are advocacy outputs in his personal orbit rather than Gates Ventures programmes. *(Flag: some individual 2020 COVAX/CEPI tranche figures come from search synthesis; the $300M CEPI and $1.6B Gavi figures are the most reliably sourced.)*

## 7. Resolve to Save Lives (RTSL)

Resolve to Save Lives is led by **Dr. Tom Frieden**, former director of the US CDC and former NYC Health Commissioner (President and CEO). It **launched in September 2017** with an initial **~$225 million, five-year commitment** from **Bloomberg Philanthropies, the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, and the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative**, and is housed within **Vital Strategies**. *(Nuance: RTSL's current site lists its funders as Bloomberg, the Gates Foundation, and Gates Philanthropy Partners "supported by the Chan Zuckerberg Foundation" — i.e. CZI's contribution was routed via Gates Philanthropy Partners. Both the launch-era "Bloomberg + Gates + CZI" framing and this routing detail are correct.)*

RTSL runs two programmes:

1. **Cardiovascular Health** — blood-pressure control, trans-fat elimination, and sodium reduction; reported reach of 34+ million people treated across 40+ countries.
2. **Epidemics / Epidemic Prevention** (formerly "Prevent Epidemics") — strengthening outbreak detection, lab networks, and rapid response across 60+ countries.

Its signature epidemic-prevention contribution is the **7-1-7 metric**: outbreaks should be **detected within 7 days, reported to authorities within 1 day, and have early response actions completed within 7 days** — the first real-time, end-to-end measure of response speed. A 2023 *Lancet Global Health* validation study across 41 outbreaks in 5 countries found roughly 1 in 4 met the full 7-1-7 target. Partners include WHO, Johns Hopkins, the CDC Foundation, the World Bank, and the Campaign for Tobacco-Free Kids; a July 2025 partnership with **PAHO** extended 7-1-7 implementation across the Americas.

## 8. Other Notable Funders

**The Audacious Project** (housed at **TED**, with **The Bridgespan Group**; launched 2018) is a **collaborative funding vehicle** that vets big nonprofit ideas and assembles multi-funder commitments at scale — roughly **$4.6 billion across 70 projects** over ~8 years (its 2026 cohort alone drew a record ~$1.03 billion). Its flagship pandemic grantee is **SENTINEL** (a **2020** project of ACEGID, Nigeria, led by Christian Happi, and the **Broad Institute**, with Pardis Sabeti) — a genomics- and CRISPR-diagnostics-based "pandemic preemption system" reported to have trained ~2,000 scientists and delivered ~250,000 diagnostic tests. *(Flags: SENTINEL's exact Audacious grant figure is not published — treat any dollar amount as unverified. Audacious also funds community-health grantees — Last Mile Health, Living Goods, Noora Health — but Target Malaria and Sherlock Biosciences were funded by Open Philanthropy, not Audacious.)*

**Patrick J. McGovern Foundation** is an **AI-and-data philanthropy** ("AI for social good") with **$1.6+ billion in assets** and recent annual grantmaking around **$66–76 million** (e.g. $75.8M across 149 grants in 13 countries in 2025), spanning health equity, crisis response, climate, and human rights. Its pandemic relevance is through the **AI-for-health / data-infrastructure** lens: it is a confirmed **Trinity Challenge funding member** and contributed **$500,000** to the Trinity Challenge on Antimicrobial Resistance to address data gaps in fighting drug resistance.

**Templeton World Charity Foundation / John Templeton Foundation** — **minimal/peripheral relevance** (flagged). These foundations fund "big questions" — human flourishing, character, science and religion — and no dedicated infectious-disease or pandemic programme was found. The only COVID-era activity identified was operational: a March 2020 John Templeton Foundation statement offering grantees timeline flexibility and reducing its grants budget. *(No specific named COVID-19 or infectious-disease research grant could be confirmed; treat as low relevance.)*

**The ELMA Foundation / ELMA Philanthropies** focuses on **African children's health**, including vaccines and immunisation. It is **named by WHO as a current funder of the mRNA Technology Transfer Programme** — the hub centred on **Afrigen Biologics in Cape Town** (with Biovac and SAMRC), launched June 2021 initially for mRNA COVID-19 vaccines (total programme funding ~US$117 million as of April 2023, alongside the EU, France, Canada, Germany, Norway, and others). ELMA also funded the **early/discovery-phase work on Afrigen's mRNA Rift Valley fever vaccine**. *(Flags: WHO publishes no standalone amount or date for ELMA's hub contribution — its specific figure is unverified. The later $6.2M to advance the RVF vaccine came from CEPI in January 2025, not ELMA — do not conflate.)*
